## Post 2 — The Complexity Tax (Week 1, Thu)
**Link:** https://petechpartners.com/blog/complexity-tax-portfolio-tech

The average PE portfolio company runs 40-60% more software applications than it needs.

It maintains 2-3 redundant systems for core business functions. And it spends 20-30% of its IT budget on integration workarounds rather than value creation.

I call this the complexity tax. It never shows up on the P&L — but it eats EBITDA every quarter and compresses exit multiples in ways that only become obvious when the next buyer does their own QofE.

Here is how it accumulates:

A bolt-on arrives with its own ERP. The team builds a bridge instead of migrating. A year later, a third bolt-on brings a third ERP. Now you have three systems, two bridges, and a reporting layer that pulls from all three to produce "consolidated" financials nobody trusts.

Each layer adds cost in three dimensions: license spend, integration maintenance, and — the biggest one — decision latency. When your board needs a unit-level margin answer on Thursday and the data team needs until Monday to reconcile it, you are paying the tax.

We have mapped this across 150+ PE-backed mid-market companies. The pattern is remarkably consistent.

**Link:** https://petechpartners.com/blog/software-graveyard-hiding-in-your-firm

$158,000 per year.

That is the median annual software waste we find when we audit enterprise software portfolios at mid-market PE firms. The range runs from $80K at lean shops to $400K+ at firms with multiple fund vintages and active portfolios.

This is not a rounding error. It is a recurring line item that compounds across fund life, and almost nobody is tracking it.

The graveyard forms the same way physical clutter does: someone buys a tool to solve a problem, the problem evolves, the person leaves, and the license auto-renews. Multiply that across every team, every portfolio onboarding, every operating partner initiative, over 5-7 years.

The most common ghosts:

— Redundant deal management tools (DealCloud + Salesforce + Excel, nobody authoritative)
— Abandoned BI licenses from a VP who left 18 months ago

— Integration middleware for a system that was decommissioned

The fix is a 4-week process, not a multi-quarter project. We publish the audit framework in the post.

## Post 4 — Cybersecurity Red Flags (Week 2, Thu)
**Link:** https://petechpartners.com/blog/cybersecurity-red-flags-target-companies

A data breach at a portfolio company is no longer a theoretical risk. It is a statistical probability.

And yet most deal teams still treat cyber diligence as a checkbox buried in the IT section of a QofE report.

That is a mistake the acquirer inherits the moment the deal closes — including every undisclosed incident, every unpatched system, and every regulatory exposure the target has been sitting on.

Five red flags I look for in every target:

1. No documented security policies. Security is ad hoc. No framework governs access, incident response, or vendor risk. You cannot assess what does not exist.

2. Shared admin credentials. One service account everybody uses. Zero auditability. First indicator of a culture that has never been forced to mature its controls.

3. No MFA on financial systems. In 2026. I still see this monthly.

4. Unpatched internet-facing infrastructure. Shodan will show you exactly what the bad guys are already looking at.

5. "We have cyber insurance, we're covered." The carrier has already decided they are not paying.

Each of these is individually survivable. The combination is a walk-away signal.

## Post 6 — The Hidden Deal Killer (Week 3, Thu)
**Link:** https://petechpartners.com/blog/hidden-deal-killer-outdated-tech

Every managing director has seen it happen.

The LOI is signed. Exclusivity is ticking. The deal team is moving fast.

Then the technology diligence report lands on the table, and the timeline craters.

Legacy technology is the single most underestimated risk in middle-market M&A. It does not show up on the balance sheet as a liability — but it functions as one.

We routinely see mid-market targets where the technology remediation cost exceeds 15% of enterprise value. That number rarely appears in the initial deal model because most deal teams lack the operational technology expertise to identify it during preliminary diligence.

The three categories that consistently blow up timelines:

1. End-of-life core systems. SAP R/3, old Oracle forms, unsupported SQL Server versions. Not only expensive to replace — they may no longer be legally compliant under current data handling regulations.

2. Custom-built systems with a single developer. Bus factor of one. When that person left in 2019, the institutional knowledge walked with them.

3. Integration-by-spreadsheet. Monthly close is run by a controller who exports three CSVs and manually reconciles them in Excel. When she retires, the close breaks.

The diligence playbook needs to identify these in the first two weeks, not week six.

## Post 7 — Step-by-Step Tech Assessment (Week 4, Tue)
**Link:** https://petechpartners.com/blog/step-by-step-tech-assessment

When a PE firm or M&A advisor engages us for a technology assessment, they want clarity on one question:

What is the real state of this company's technology, and what will it cost to get it where it needs to be?

Not a laundry list. A defensible number they can plug into the deal model.

Here is the framework, refined across hundreds of engagements:

**Phase 1 — Inventory.** Every system, every integration, every license. We go beyond what the target's IT team can produce from memory. Four domains: core apps, infrastructure, security, data.

**Phase 2 — Risk overlay.** Map each system against business criticality, end-of-life timeline, and compliance exposure. This is where the walk-away signals surface.

**Phase 3 — Integration readiness.** How hard is it to plug this into the platform company? APIs vs file drops vs manual re-entry determine whether the 100-day plan is realistic or fiction.

**Phase 4 — Cost to remediate.** Three scenarios — minimal viable, platform standard, and transformation — each with a defensible number and a timeline.

You get a 20-page report that your operating partner can hand to the board without edits. And a spreadsheet that the deal model plugs into directly.

## Post 8 — AI-Risk Diligence / Walk Away Factor (Week 4, Thu)
**Link:** https://petechpartners.com/blog/ai-risk-diligence-walk-away-factor-best-practice-guide-pe-ma-firms-2026

There is a capability gap forming in private equity right now.

On one side: firms using AI-enabled diligence to confidently kill bad deals early.
On the other: firms bleeding analyst hours chasing transactions they should have exited weeks before LOI.

The difference is what I call the Walk Away Factor — a defined threshold at which identified risks objectively justify exiting a deal, based on data signals rather than gut feel or sunk-cost pressure.

Three things make this possible in 2026 that were not possible in 2023:

1. Document-level anomaly detection. AI now flags inconsistencies between the CIM, data room, and public filings in hours, not weeks.

2. Integrated workflow automation. Diligence trackers, CRM, and pipeline dashboards share state. Red flags propagate instantly instead of sitting in a consultant's inbox.

3. The lean team multiplier. A 4-person deal team running connected automation covers more ground than a 12-person team using disconnected tools.

The firms adopting this are walking away from 20% more deals — and closing a higher percentage of the ones they pursue. That is not a productivity gain. That is a returns profile change.
