Here is the uncomfortable math: the average cost of a data breach hit $4.88M last year, according to IBM's Cost of a Data Breach Report. For a portfolio company running at a 15% EBITDA margin, that single incident wipes out the profitability of roughly $32.5M in revenue. On a 10x multiple, it erases nearly half a billion dollars of enterprise value at exit. And yet, in diligence after diligence, we keep seeing the same pattern: cybersecurity and AI governance are treated as IT line items rather than value-creation levers.

If you are a private equity operator, an M&A advisor, or a leadership team member at a sponsor-backed business, this post is for you. The threat surface has changed. The diligence playbook has not. And the gap between the two is where value is quietly leaking out of portfolios today.

## The New Pain: Your Portfolio Is One Unpatched AI Tool Away From an EBITDA-Destroying Breach

Three years ago, a CISO could credibly say, "We have an EDR, an MSP, and cyber insurance — we're covered." That answer no longer holds. Here's what changed:

**Shadow AI is now everywhere.** Employees at portfolio companies are pasting customer data, financial models, and source code into public LLMs dozens of times per day. A 2025 survey from Cyberhaven found 27% of data employees put into generative AI tools was sensitive — up from 10.7% the year prior. None of it shows up on the CFO's risk register.

**Attackers have AI too.** Phishing campaigns are now hyper-personalized at scale. Deepfake CFO voice calls authorizing wire transfers are no longer hypothetical — Arup lost $25M to exactly that attack in Hong Kong. Your controller does not know what they cannot verify.

**Insurance is narrowing, not widening.** Cyber carriers are excluding AI-related incidents, tightening ransomware sublimits, and requiring MFA attestations that many mid-market companies cannot truthfully sign. A denied claim after an incident is the second-worst call an operating partner can take.

**Regulators are catching up.** The SEC's cyber disclosure rules, the EU AI Act, and state-level privacy laws now create reporting obligations that can trigger material adverse change clauses in sale agreements. A quiet breach is no longer an option.

Put these together and the conclusion is simple: cybersecurity and AI risk are now operating metrics. They belong next to net revenue retention and gross margin on the monthly operating review.

## The Proof: Do the Math on Your Own Portfolio

We ran this calculation for a recent client — a $120M ARR software platform held by a mid-market sponsor. The board had approved $380K in annual security spend. Felt reasonable. Then we modeled the exposure:

- Probability of a material incident over a 3-year hold: ~29% (IBM/Ponemon baseline for their sector and size)
- Expected loss per incident: $4.88M direct + ~$3.1M in customer churn and deal delays
- Exit multiple compression if the breach hit during a sale process: 0.8x–1.2x turns
- Expected value of the risk: roughly $14M over the hold period

Against that, a hardened program — zero-trust identity, AI usage governance, 24/7 detection, tabletop-tested incident response — runs around $900K/year all-in. Net expected value: over $11M in protected enterprise value per platform company, before you count the multiple expansion from a clean cyber diligence at exit.

That is not an IT conversation. That is a value-creation thesis.

## What "Good" Looks Like in 2026

If you want a practical checklist for your next portfolio review, these are the five things we look for first:

1. **A named AI Acceptable Use Policy** that is actually enforced at the browser and endpoint, not just published in an employee handbook.
2. **Identity as the perimeter** — phishing-resistant MFA (FIDO2, not SMS), conditional access, and privileged access management on every admin account.
3. **A tested incident response runbook** with legal, PR, and insurance contacts pre-wired, and at least one tabletop exercise per year.
4. **Third-party and vendor AI risk** reviewed the same way you review financial covenants — because your customers are starting to ask.
5. **Exit-ready documentation** so that cyber and AI posture accelerate the sale instead of stalling it in the data room.

None of this is exotic. All of it is missing, in whole or in part, in the majority of mid-market portfolio companies we assess.

## The Operating Partner's Move

The operators who are winning right now are the ones treating this as a 100-day playbook item, not a fire drill after the first incident. They are:

- Adding a single cyber + AI risk KPI to the monthly CEO scorecard.
- Running a 5-day rapid assessment across the portfolio so the sponsor has one comparable view.
- Tying CISO or MSP compensation to measurable outcomes — mean time to detect, patch SLA, phishing failure rate — not activity.
- Pre-building the exit-ready cyber diligence package from day one of the hold.

That shift alone — from reactive IT vendor management to proactive operating discipline — is worth the engagement before we write a single line of technical recommendation.
