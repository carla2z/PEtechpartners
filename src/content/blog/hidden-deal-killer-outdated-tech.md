Every managing director has seen it happen. The LOI is signed, exclusivity is ticking, and the deal team is moving fast. Then the technology due diligence report lands on the table, and the timeline craters.

Legacy technology is the single most underestimated risk in middle-market M&A. It does not show up on the balance sheet as a liability, but it functions as one. When a target company is running its core operations on a patchwork of end-of-life software, unsupported databases, and manual workarounds, the integration timeline and cost projections built into the deal model become unreliable.

## The Real Cost of Technical Debt in M&A

Technical debt compounds silently. A target company running SAP R/3 alongside a custom-built CRM from 2009 and a file-server-based document management system is not just behind the curve. It is carrying integration risk that directly impacts post-close value creation.

We routinely see mid-market targets where the technology remediation cost exceeds 15% of the enterprise value. That number rarely appears in the initial deal model because most deal teams lack the operational technology expertise to identify it during preliminary diligence.

The specific risks break down into three categories:

**Integration timeline expansion.** Outdated systems resist modern integration patterns. API-first platforms can be connected in weeks. Legacy monoliths require custom middleware, data migration projects, and parallel-run periods that stretch timelines by 6 to 18 months.

**Hidden operational fragility.** Many legacy environments depend on single points of failure, whether that is an unsupported operating system, a database version with known vulnerabilities, or institutional knowledge held by one or two employees. These dependencies create execution risk that surfaces only after close.

**Valuation compression.** Sophisticated buyers and their operating partners now factor technology maturity into their valuation frameworks. A target running modern, composable infrastructure commands a premium over a comparable business with a legacy stack, even at identical revenue multiples.

## What Slows Deals Down

The due diligence process itself breaks when the target cannot produce basic technology documentation. We see this pattern repeatedly: deal teams request a systems inventory, network architecture diagram, or data flow map, and the target responds with incomplete spreadsheets and outdated Visio files.

This forces the acquirer into an extended discovery phase, which consumes exclusivity windows and strains deal team resources. In competitive auctions, this delay can be fatal to a bid.

The sell-side advisory community is beginning to recognize this problem. Forward-thinking investment banks now recommend that sellers complete a [technology assessment](/solutions) before going to market, specifically to avoid the discovery delays that kill deal momentum.

## Practical Steps for Deal Teams

For buy-side teams, the solution is integrating technology diligence earlier in the process. Do not wait until confirmatory diligence to engage a technology advisor. A preliminary [tech assessment](/tools) during the IOI phase surfaces the material risks before you commit capital to full diligence.

For sell-side preparation, the calculus is straightforward. Every dollar invested in modernizing critical systems before a process generates a measurable return through faster diligence cycles, cleaner data rooms, and stronger buyer confidence.

The firms that consistently close transactions on timeline are the ones that treat technology infrastructure as a first-class diligence workstream, not an afterthought delegated to the IT department.
