The number in the title is not hypothetical. It is the median annual waste we find when we audit enterprise software portfolios at mid-market private equity firms and M&A advisory practices. The range runs from $80,000 at lean operations to well over $400,000 at firms with multiple fund vintages and portfolio companies.

This is not a rounding error. It is a recurring line item that compounds across fund life, and almost nobody is tracking it.

## How Software Graveyards Form

Enterprise software accumulates the same way physical clutter does. Somebody purchases a tool to solve a specific problem. The problem evolves or the person leaves, but the license renews automatically. Multiply this pattern across every team, every portfolio company onboarding, and every operating partner initiative over five to seven years, and you have a graveyard.

The most common categories of dead spend we encounter:

**Redundant deal management tools.** The deal team adopted DealCloud three years ago, but half the associates still use a combination of Salesforce and Excel. Both subscriptions persist. Neither system contains a complete picture of the pipeline.

**Orphaned portfolio monitoring platforms.** A previous operating partner championed a specific BI tool for portfolio reporting. That partner moved on. The portfolio companies never fully adopted it. The enterprise license renewed twice since then.

**Overlapping communication and collaboration suites.** Microsoft 365 and Google Workspace running in parallel. Slack and Teams both active. Zoom and Webex both licensed. Each has its advocates, and nobody has made the call to consolidate.

**Unused security and compliance tools.** A compliance officer purchased a GRC platform after a regulatory scare. The initial implementation stalled at 30% completion. The annual renewal is $45,000.

## The Compounding Effect on Fund Economics

For a firm managing $2 billion across three funds, $158,000 in annual software waste might seem immaterial. But the downstream effects are not.

First, redundant tools fragment data. When deal intelligence lives across three platforms, no single system provides the pipeline visibility that LPs expect. This creates manual reconciliation work that consumes analyst time, typically 8 to 12 hours per week across the deal team.

Second, overlapping systems create security surface area. Every active SaaS application is an authentication endpoint, a data repository, and a potential breach vector. Unused tools with active credentials are particularly dangerous because nobody is monitoring them.

Third, the operational complexity of managing a bloated software stack drains IT and operations bandwidth that should be directed at value creation initiatives across the portfolio.

## The Audit Process

Our [technology assessment](/solutions) for PE firms follows a structured approach. We start with a complete SaaS discovery scan, pulling subscription data from finance systems, SSO providers, and browser-based usage analytics. This typically surfaces 30 to 40% more active subscriptions than the firm is aware of.

We then layer utilization data on top of the subscription inventory. A tool with 200 seats and 11 active users in the last 90 days tells a clear story. We classify every application into four categories: essential and well-adopted, essential but underutilized, redundant, and fully abandoned.

The remediation roadmap prioritizes quick wins first. Contract terminations and downgrades that can execute within the current billing cycle, followed by consolidation projects that require migration planning.
