A data breach at a portfolio company is no longer a theoretical risk. It is a statistical probability. The average cost of a breach continues to climb, and regulatory enforcement is tightening across every jurisdiction. For PE firms acquiring mid-market businesses, cybersecurity due diligence is no longer optional.

Yet most deal teams still treat cyber as a checkbox item, buried in the IT section of a QofE report. This is a mistake. The acquirer inherits the target's security posture, its regulatory exposure, and any undisclosed incidents the moment the deal closes.

Here are the red flags we look for, and what they mean for deal risk.

## No Documented Security Policies

When a target company cannot produce written security policies, it tells us that security decisions are ad hoc. There is no consistent framework governing access controls, incident response, data handling, or vendor risk management.

This does not necessarily mean the company has been breached. It means there is no verifiable way to assess whether adequate controls exist. For a deal team, that uncertainty translates directly into integration cost and timeline risk.

## Flat Network Architecture

A flat network, where every device can communicate with every other device, is one of the clearest indicators of immature security infrastructure. Modern environments segment networks to contain lateral movement in the event of a compromise.

If the target's production database, employee workstations, and guest WiFi all share the same network segment, the blast radius of any single compromised endpoint extends to the entire environment.

## No Multi-Factor Authentication

MFA is the single highest-impact security control available, and it is table stakes in 2026. When a target company does not enforce MFA on email, VPN, and critical business applications, it signals systemic underinvestment in security fundamentals.

We see this most often in companies that grew quickly without a dedicated security function. The initial IT setup was convenience-oriented, and nobody revisited the authentication architecture as the company scaled.

## Outdated or Missing Endpoint Protection

Legacy antivirus running signature-based detection on Windows endpoints is not adequate protection. We look for modern endpoint detection and response platforms that provide behavioral analysis and centralized visibility.

The absence of EDR tooling is a risk factor, but the more concerning finding is when a company has deployed security tools but is not monitoring them. An unmonitored SIEM or an EDR console that nobody reviews is security theater.

## No Incident Response Plan

Ask the target what happens when they detect a security incident. If the answer involves improvisation rather than a documented, tested playbook, the company is not prepared for the inevitable.

We evaluate whether the target has a written incident response plan, whether it has been tested within the last 12 months, and whether the plan includes communication protocols for regulators, customers, and the board.

## Regulatory Compliance Gaps

Depending on the industry, the target may be subject to HIPAA, PCI-DSS, SOC 2, GDPR, or state-level privacy regulations. We verify not just whether the company claims compliance, but whether the evidence supports it. Expired certifications, incomplete audit trails, and undocumented data processing activities are common findings.

## What This Means for Your Deal

Each of these red flags has a quantifiable remediation cost. A missing MFA deployment might cost $30,000 to implement. A network segmentation project could run $150,000 or more. The aggregate remediation cost needs to factor into the deal model, either as a purchase price adjustment or a post-close capital expenditure.
